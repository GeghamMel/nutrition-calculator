Methodology

How the calculator works

We follow the same formulas used by registered dietitians and sports nutritionists.

1

Calculate BMR

Basal Metabolic Rate — the calories your body needs at complete rest — is calculated using the Mifflin-St Jeor equation.

2

Apply activity multiplier

BMR is multiplied by your activity factor to get TDEE — Total Daily Energy Expenditure, or your maintenance calories.

3

Adjust for your goal

We apply a 15–20% deficit for fat loss or a 10–15% surplus for muscle gain, based on evidence-based guidelines.

4

Split into macros

Calories are split into protein, carbohydrates, and fat using goal-specific ratios to support your outcome.

Common questions

Frequently asked questions

BMR (Basal Metabolic Rate) is the number of calories your body burns at rest to maintain basic functions like breathing and circulation. TDEE (Total Daily Energy Expenditure) includes your BMR plus the calories you burn through daily activity and exercise. TDEE is the number you should use for nutrition planning.
The Mifflin-St Jeor formula used here is considered one of the most accurate BMR equations available, with an average error of around 10% for healthy adults. However, individual metabolism can vary due to muscle mass, hormones, and genetics. Use the result as a starting point and adjust by 100–150 kcal based on your real-world results after 2–3 weeks.
The general evidence-based recommendation for active individuals is 1.6–2.2 grams of protein per kilogram of body weight. The calculator sets protein to approximately 2g/kg for muscle gain and fat loss goals, and slightly lower for maintenance. You can read our full protein guide for more detail.
If you have never tracked calories before, it can be helpful to eat at maintenance for 1–2 weeks first. This gives you a real-world baseline, helps you understand your eating habits, and makes the transition to a deficit much smoother and more sustainable.
This calculator is designed for generally healthy adults. If you have a medical condition such as diabetes, kidney disease, thyroid disorders, or are pregnant, please consult a registered dietitian or your healthcare provider before making significant dietary changes.
