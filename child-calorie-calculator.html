Children's nutrition

Why children need different calories

1

Growth requires extra energy

Children are building new tissues — bone, muscle, and organs — constantly. This growth process demands additional calories beyond what is needed just for daily activity.

2

Higher metabolic rate per kg

Children have a significantly higher metabolic rate per kilogram of body weight compared to adults. A 10-year-old typically burns 70–80 kcal per kg per day, versus 25–35 kcal/kg for adults.

3

Protein needs are proportionally higher

Children need more protein per kg of body weight than adults to support growth. The recommendation ranges from 1.0–1.5g/kg depending on age — higher than the adult RDA of 0.8g/kg.

4

Never put children on adult diets

Restricting calories in children can impair growth and brain development. If concerned about your child's weight, always consult a paediatrician rather than restricting food independently.

Methodology

How this estimate is calculated

This calculator uses Estimated Energy Requirement (EER) equations for children and adolescents, developed by the U.S. Institute of Medicine (now the National Academy of Medicine), which factor in age, weight, height, and physical activity level. Unlike adult calorie formulas, the paediatric equations include an additional energy allowance specifically for growth, since children are actively building new tissue rather than just maintaining an existing body.

Children's nutritional needs vary enormously with growth spurts, puberty timing, and individual metabolism, far more than adult needs vary between individuals of similar age. This tool provides a population-level estimate, not a personalised medical assessment. We strongly recommend using this as general information alongside — not instead of — regular check-ups with a paediatrician, who can track your child's individual growth curve over time, which is a far more reliable indicator of healthy development than any single calorie number.
