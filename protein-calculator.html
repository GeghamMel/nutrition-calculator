Protein science

Why protein needs vary by sport

1

Strength athletes need the most

Resistance training causes muscle breakdown — rebuilding needs 1.6–2.4g/kg.

2

Endurance athletes also benefit

Endurance training also increases protein needs to 1.2–1.6g/kg.

3

Higher needs when cutting

During cutting, increase protein to 2.0–2.4g/kg to preserve muscle.

4

Spread protein across 4–5 meals

30–50g protein per meal maximises muscle protein synthesis.

Methodology

Where these numbers come from

These recommendations are based on a body of sports nutrition research, most notably the position stands from the International Society of Sports Nutrition (ISSN) and systematic reviews by researchers like Dr. Brad Schoenfeld and Dr. Stuart Phillips, who have studied protein intake and muscle protein synthesis extensively. The widely cited range of 1.6–2.2 g/kg for muscle building comes from meta-analyses pooling dozens of controlled trials — above roughly 2.2 g/kg, additional protein shows minimal further benefit for muscle growth in most trained individuals.

Keep in mind these figures assume adequate total calorie intake and resistance training stimulus — protein alone doesn't build muscle without a training signal to use it for. People with kidney disease should consult a doctor before significantly increasing protein intake, since very high intakes may stress impaired kidneys. For most healthy adults, though, research has found no evidence that intakes in the 1.6–2.4 g/kg range pose any risk to kidney or bone health.
