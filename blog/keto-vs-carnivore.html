Keto vs Carnivore Diet — What's the Difference?

8 min read · Nutrition · Updated July 2026

Carnivore is sometimes described as "keto taken to its logical extreme," and there's real truth to that framing — but the two diets differ in more than just degree. One is built around a specific metabolic state; the other is built around eliminating an entire category of food regardless of macros. Understanding that distinction matters more than the surface-level similarity of "both are very low-carb."

What each diet actually restricts

Keto restricts carbohydrates to roughly 20-50g net carbs per day, typically achieved through a diet of meat, fish, eggs, low-carb vegetables, nuts, and full-fat dairy, with the goal of shifting the body into ketosis — burning fat and ketones for fuel instead of glucose. Carnivore eliminates all plant foods entirely, restricting intake to meat, fish, eggs, and in some versions, limited dairy — carbohydrate restriction is a side effect of this elimination rather than the primary goal.

The goal is different, not just the strictness

This is the key distinction people often miss. Keto is explicitly designed around achieving and maintaining ketosis, with carb restriction as the mechanism to get there — vegetables, nuts, and low-carb fruits are all permitted precisely because they fit within the carb budget needed to stay in ketosis. Carnivore's rationale isn't built around ketosis specifically; it's built around the idea that eliminating plant foods entirely removes potential irritants or anti-nutrients some proponents believe cause inflammation or digestive issues, with any resulting ketosis being incidental rather than the primary target.

Food variety and practical sustainability

Keto, despite its restrictions, still allows a meaningfully varied diet — different vegetables, nuts, cheeses, and preparation styles keep meals varied day to day. Carnivore's food list is dramatically narrower, limited to animal products only, which some people find simplifies decision-making but others find becomes monotonous and harder to sustain over months rather than weeks.

Nutrient completeness: where each diet faces scrutiny

Keto's inclusion of vegetables and nuts provides fibre, vitamin C, and a range of micronutrients that are harder to obtain purely from animal sources. Carnivore's complete elimination of plant foods raises genuine questions about fibre intake (essentially zero) and certain nutrients traditionally associated with fruits and vegetables — proponents argue organ meats and the elimination of plant "anti-nutrients" offset this, but this remains a contested claim without the same weight of long-term research behind it that more established diets have.

What the evidence actually supports

Keto has a substantial body of research, particularly for epilepsy management (its original clinical use) and more recent studies on weight loss and blood sugar control. Carnivore has comparatively little formal research — most evidence is anecdotal, from online communities and self-reported case studies, rather than controlled clinical trials. This doesn't mean carnivore doesn't work for the individuals who report benefits from it, but it does mean the evidence base is considerably thinner than keto's.

Who tends to choose each approach

People drawn to keto are often motivated by specific, well-documented goals — weight loss, blood sugar management, or simply wanting more food variety within a low-carb framework. People drawn to carnivore are often looking to address specific digestive or inflammatory symptoms they believe are triggered by plant compounds, or want the simplicity of a very narrow, rule-based approach without needing to track vegetables, nuts, or precise carb counts.

Which one should you try?

If you want the metabolic benefits associated with ketosis while maintaining reasonable food variety and a stronger evidence base, keto is the more conservative, better-studied starting point. If you've already tried standard low-carb approaches without resolving specific symptoms and are curious about a stricter elimination approach, carnivore is worth discussing with a doctor first, given its more extreme restriction and thinner research base — this isn't a diet to adopt casually without medical input, particularly for extended periods.

Frequently asked questions

Is carnivore just a stricter version of keto?

In practice yes — carnivore is naturally very low in carbs since it excludes all plant foods, but it was not designed around achieving ketosis specifically the way keto was originally built to do.

Can you get enough vitamin C on carnivore?

This is genuinely debated. Organ meats contain some vitamin C, and very low carbohydrate intake reduces the amount some researchers believe is needed, but this remains a contested claim without strong long-term evidence either way.

Which diet is easier to sustain long-term?

Keto generally offers more food variety and flexibility, which tends to make it easier for most people to sustain over months and years compared to carnivore's much narrower food list.

Do I need to fully eliminate carbs on keto the way carnivore does?

No — keto typically allows 20-50g of net carbs daily from low-carb vegetables, nuts, and some dairy, while carnivore eliminates plant foods and their carbohydrates entirely.
